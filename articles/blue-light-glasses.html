Blue Light Glasses: Do They Actually Work?

Lenses 10 min read April 2026

In This Article

Blue light glasses have become one of the most talked-about accessories of the digital age. With the average Canadian spending over 11 hours a day in front of screens, it's no surprise that products claiming to protect our eyes have surged in popularity. But do blue light glasses actually do what they promise?

This guide breaks down the science, separates the marketing claims from the evidence, and helps you decide whether blue light filtering lenses are worth adding to your next pair of glasses.

What Is Blue Light?

Blue light is part of the visible light spectrum, with wavelengths between approximately 380nm and 500nm. It's emitted by the sun (which is by far the largest source), as well as by digital screens, LED lighting, and fluorescent bulbs.

Not all blue light is harmful. In fact, blue-turquoise light (between 450-500nm) is essential for regulating our circadian rhythm — the internal clock that tells our body when to sleep and wake. The concern is specifically about high-energy blue-violet light (380-450nm), which some researchers suggest may contribute to eye strain and retinal stress over prolonged exposure.

Key Fact

Your screen emits significantly less blue light than the sun. A sunny day delivers roughly 100 times more blue light exposure than an hour of screen time. The concern with screens is about duration and proximity, not intensity.

What Do Blue Light Glasses Do?

Blue light glasses use special coatings or lens materials to filter out a percentage of blue light before it reaches your eyes. Depending on the lens technology, they can block anywhere from 10% to 90% of blue light in the 400-450nm range.

There are two main types:

  • Blue light coating — A thin filter applied to the surface of the lens. This is what most optical retailers offer, including same-day blue cut lenses at stores like Charm Optical. These typically filter 20-40% of blue light and have minimal colour distortion.
  • Blue light lens material — The filtering is embedded directly into the lens material itself. These tend to have a slight yellow tint and filter more aggressively (40-70%).

What Does the Science Say?

This is where it gets nuanced. The research on blue light glasses falls into two main areas: eye strain and sleep quality.

Blue Light and Eye Strain

The largest systematic review to date, published by the Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews in 2023, analyzed six randomized controlled trials involving 343 participants. Their conclusion: there is currently insufficient evidence that blue light filtering lenses reduce eye fatigue compared to non-filtering lenses.

However, this doesn't mean blue light glasses are useless. The review noted that most studies were small and short-term. Many eye care professionals, including opticians and optometrists across Canada, still recommend blue light coatings as part of a broader approach to managing screen-related discomfort.

The reality is that digital eye strain (also called computer vision syndrome) is likely caused by multiple factors working together:

  • Reduced blink rate when staring at screens (blink rate drops by up to 60%)
  • Poor screen distance and angle
  • Inadequate lighting in the workspace
  • Uncorrected or under-corrected refractive errors in your prescription
  • Prolonged near-focus work without breaks

Blue light may be one contributing factor, but it's rarely the sole cause of eye strain.

Blue Light and Sleep

The evidence for blue light's impact on sleep is more compelling. Multiple studies have shown that exposure to blue light in the evening hours suppresses melatonin production — the hormone that signals your body to prepare for sleep.

A 2019 study published in Chronobiology International found that participants who wore blue light filtering glasses for 2 hours before bedtime experienced:

  • 58% increase in melatonin levels compared to the control group
  • Improved subjective sleep quality
  • Faster time to fall asleep

If you're a heavy evening screen user — scrolling your phone in bed, watching TV late, or working on a laptop after dinner — blue light glasses may genuinely improve your sleep quality.

The most effective approach combines multiple strategies. Start with the 20-20-20 rule (every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds), ensure your prescription is current with a comprehensive eye exam, and consider blue light coating as an added layer of comfort.

Who Should Consider Blue Light Glasses?

While not medically necessary for everyone, blue light glasses may benefit:

  • Heavy screen users — If you spend 8+ hours daily on screens for work, adding blue light coating to your prescription glasses is a low-cost addition that many wearers report helps with end-of-day eye fatigue.
  • Evening screen users — If you use screens within 2-3 hours of bedtime and struggle with sleep, blue light filtering has the strongest scientific backing in this context.
  • Children and teens — With increasing screen time in schools and at home, many parents are opting for blue light protection for their kids' glasses. Myopia rates are rising in Canadian children, and while blue light isn't a direct cause, reducing overall digital eye stress is a reasonable precaution.
  • Post-cataract surgery patients — Natural lenses filter some blue light; after cataract removal, artificial intraocular lenses may let more blue light through, making blue light glasses more beneficial for this group.

How to Choose Blue Light Glasses

If you decide to try blue light glasses, here's what to look for:

1. Decide: Prescription or Non-Prescription

If you already wear glasses, the easiest route is adding a blue light coating to your current prescription lenses. Most optical shops offer this as an add-on — for example, Charm Optical in Edmonton offers same-day blue cut lenses that combine blue light filtering with anti-reflective coating.

If you don't need prescription lenses, you can buy non-prescription (plano) blue light glasses from most optical retailers or online. Just make sure they have a clear return policy in case you don't notice a difference.

2. Check the Filtering Percentage

Not all blue light lenses are equal. Ask about the percentage of blue light filtered in the 400-450nm range. For daytime use, 20-40% filtering is usually sufficient. For evening use specifically to help sleep, a higher filtering percentage (40-60%) may be more effective, though these lenses tend to have a more noticeable yellow tint.

3. Consider the Lens Quality

A cheap pair of blue light glasses from a dollar store and a pair from a licensed optician are not the same product. Professional-grade blue light lenses typically also include:

  • Anti-reflective coating (reduces screen glare)
  • Scratch resistance
  • UV protection
  • Hydrophobic coating (resists smudges)

When you're investing in lenses you'll wear daily, the quality of the optics matters. A comprehensive eye exam at a local optical store ensures your lenses are properly fitted to your prescription, pupillary distance, and frame choice.

4. Pick the Right Frame

Blue light coating can be applied to virtually any frame — from classic Ray-Ban styles to Oakley sport frames to designer Prada eyeglasses. The coating itself is invisible and doesn't change the appearance of the frame, so you can choose based on style preference.

The Bottom Line

Blue light glasses are not a miracle product, but they're not snake oil either. Here's the honest summary:

  • For eye strain: The evidence is weak, but many users report subjective improvement. They work best as part of a broader strategy (breaks, proper prescription, good lighting).
  • For sleep: The evidence is stronger. If you use screens in the evening, blue light filtering before bed may genuinely help.
  • For eye health: There is currently no evidence that screen-level blue light causes permanent eye damage. Your sunglasses on a bright day matter more for UV/blue light protection than your screen glasses.

If you're considering blue light glasses, talk to a licensed optician about adding blue light coating to your next pair. It's a relatively small investment — and if you spend most of your day on screens, the potential comfort benefit outweighs the modest cost.
